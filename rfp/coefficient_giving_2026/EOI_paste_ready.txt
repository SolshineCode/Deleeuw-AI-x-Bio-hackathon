Refusal depth measures whether an AI model's stated refusal is structurally backed by internal suppression of hazard representations or just papered over intact capability. Every major AI biosecurity benchmark measures whether a model produces hazardous output. None measure refusal depth. A synthesis-screening classifier whose refusal sits one formatting shift from compliance is, for governance purposes, not really a classifier.

The gap shows up across attack vectors. On Gemma 4 E2B-IT, 65 of 75 bio-hazard prompts produce refusals with standard chat-template formatting. Strip one chat-template token and that drops to zero. The hazard capability stays intact behind a token-gated classifier. On Gemma 2 2B-IT we found zero genuine refusals, only universal hedging. At 80-token generation caps (a common API default), both models refuse zero percent. Standard evaluations like VCT, WMDP-Bio, ABC-Bench, and LAB-Bench report both models as safe. They are not.

BioRefusalAudit operationalizes this as divergence (D) between surface output and internal SAE feature activations. Low D indicates internal non-activation of hazard features. High D indicates thin policy layer over intact capability. This matches Sandbrink's AIxBio 2026 call for monitoring AI-bio interactions without reading content. Auditing activations rather than prompts or completions flags shallow refusals while preserving user privacy.

Pipeline runs on a 4 GB consumer GPU with a 75-prompt HL3-licensed eval set. It is causally validated. On Gemma 2, ablate/boost on top features flipped labels or shifted D by 0.2+ in 80 percent of pairs. A Schedule I confound surfaced. Refusal circuits track cultural salience more than CBRN risk, and psilocybin gets refused more often than hazard-adjacent biology.
